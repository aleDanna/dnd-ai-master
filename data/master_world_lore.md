# DM World & Lore Handbook (for the AI Master)

> Source: distilled from the D&D 5e Dungeon Master's Guide (2024), chapters 4-7 (Adventures, Campaigns, Cosmology, Treasure & Magic Items) plus the Lore Glossary. Companion to `master_handbook.md` (DM craft). This document gives the master the in-world knowledge needed to ground a campaign — cosmology, magic, cultures, factions, common tropes — so worldbuilding stays consistent and evocative.

---

## 1. The Multiverse (Cosmology)

The default D&D cosmology is called the **Great Wheel** — concentric rings of planes around a central Material Plane. Use it as the default reference; deviate only if the campaign explicitly establishes another model.

### 1.1 The Five Plane Categories

- **Material Realms** — the Material Plane (where most adventures play out) plus its two echoes:
  - **Feywild** — wondrous, whimsical, vivid. Time runs strangely; promises bind. Home of fey, archfey, eladrin.
  - **Shadowfell** — bleak, ominous, drained of color. Home of shadar-kai, undead, the Raven Queen.
- **Transitive Planes** — boundless passageways between other planes:
  - **Ethereal Plane** — a misty border realm; ghosts and bordering between the Material and Inner planes.
  - **Astral Plane** — a silver void of thought; githyanki and the divine corpses of dead gods drift here.
- **Inner Planes** — the four **Elemental Planes** (Air, Earth, Fire, Water), plus para-elementals at their borders, dissolving outward into the **Elemental Chaos**. Source of raw matter and energy. Home of genies, elementals, salamanders, mephits.
- **Outer Planes** — seventeen alignment-themed realms of pure thought and spirit. Home of celestials (Upper Planes), fiends (Lower Planes), and stranger entities (modrons in Mechanus, slaadi in Limbo).
- **Positive and Negative Planes** — enfold the entire cosmology, providing raw life and death energies.

### 1.2 The Outer Planes (memorize these)

| Plane | Alignment | Notable feature |
|---|---|---|
| Mount Celestia | LG | seven sacred plateaus, archons |
| Bytopia | LG/NG | twin paradises facing each other |
| Elysium | NG | the Blessed Fields, source of the River Oceanus |
| Beastlands | NG/CG | wilderness of beast-spirits |
| Arborea | CG | wild romance, eladrin courts |
| Ysgard | CG/CN | endless heroic warfare; combatants resurrect at dawn |
| Limbo | CN | raw chaos shaped by force of will, slaadi |
| Pandemonium | CE/CN | howling caverns, eternal wind |
| The Abyss | CE | infinite layers of demons, chaos, evil |
| Carceri | CE/NE | nested prison-spheres, traitors and betrayers |
| Hades | NE | the Three Glooms, despair |
| Gehenna | LE/NE | volcanic mountains, yugoloths' homeland |
| The Nine Hells | LE | nine descending tiers of organized evil; Asmodeus rules |
| Acheron | LE/LN | colliding cubes of war |
| Mechanus | LN | clockwork order, modrons, perfect law |
| Arcadia | LG/LN | structured paradise, peregrines |
| The Outlands | N | the neutral hub; the city of **Sigil** sits at its peak |

When narrating planar references, use the right alignment flavor: a celestial speaks of duty and light; a devil of contracts and order; a demon of hunger and chaos.

### 1.3 Sigil, the City of Doors

A torus-shaped city floating above the Outlands' infinite spire, ruled by the inscrutable **Lady of Pain**. Sigil features doorways to every plane — many keyed to a specific item or condition. The default cross-planar trade hub. Use it as a setting for "anything goes" cosmopolitan adventures.

### 1.4 Planar Travel Methods

- **Portals** — fixed gates between two planes; often keyed (key item, command word, situation, time, or random).
- **Spells** — Plane Shift, Gate, Astral Projection, Etherealness, Banishment.
- **Magic items** — Cubic Gate, Well of Many Worlds, Talisman of the Sphere.
- **Conduits** — the **River Styx** flows through the Lower Planes; the **River Oceanus** through the Upper; the **Infinite Staircase** connects everywhere; **Yggdrasil** the World Tree threads Ysgard, the Material, and Niflheim.

### 1.5 Planar Dissonance

A celestial that lingers on a Lower Plane (or a fiend on an Upper Plane) for more than a few hours starts feeling sick. After every long rest on the wrong plane, DC 10 Con save; on a fail, subtract 1d4 from every D20 roll. Cumulative; ends after a long rest on a compatible plane. Use this when the player asks "what happens if a devil hangs around in Mount Celestia?"

### 1.6 Time on Other Planes

Time can run strangely:
- **Feywild** — a year inside can be a decade outside, or vice versa. Mortals lose memories of mortal life if they linger.
- **Outer Planes** — distance is conceptual. The same plane can feel small one trip and infinite the next; the plane decides.
- **Astral Plane** — no aging, no hunger, no thirst; a meditative state of timelessness.

## 2. Magic in the World

### 2.1 The Three Sources

Magic in D&D flows from three primal sources. Most spellcasters tap one (sometimes blended):

- **Arcane** — manipulating the Weave, the magical fabric overlying reality. Wizards study it; sorcerers are born to it; warlocks are gifted it. Bards weave it into music. Highly intellectual and willful.
- **Divine** — granted by deities, celestial patrons, or moral force. Clerics serve a god or domain; paladins channel oaths; some warlocks (celestial pact) tap the same wellspring.
- **Primal** — the raw life of nature, beasts, weather, and primordial spirits. Druids are its conduits; rangers its applied practitioners.

### 2.2 The Weave

The Weave is the underlying lattice of arcane magic on the Material Plane (often guarded by the goddess Mystra in the Forgotten Realms; analogous deities exist elsewhere). When a spell is cast, the caster shapes the Weave. Wild Magic and broken regions of the Weave (dead-magic zones, wild-magic zones) are common adventure flavor.

### 2.3 Schools of Magic

Each spell belongs to one of eight schools — guidance for narrative coloring:

- **Abjuration** — protection, wards, banishment, dispelling
- **Conjuration** — summoning, teleportation, creating matter
- **Divination** — knowledge, scrying, prediction
- **Enchantment** — charming, controlling minds, sleeping
- **Evocation** — raw energy: fire, lightning, force, healing
- **Illusion** — deceiving the senses
- **Necromancy** — life and death, undead, draining
- **Transmutation** — changing form, properties, matter

When narrating spell effects, lean into the school's flavor. Evocation feels physical and immediate; illusion feels uncanny and dreamlike; necromancy feels cold and wrong.

### 2.4 Magic Items

Every magic item has a **rarity** that reflects power and availability:

| Rarity | Typical level | Sale price (gp) |
|---|---|---|
| Common | 1+ | ~100 |
| Uncommon | 1+ | ~400 |
| Rare | 5+ | ~4,000 |
| Very Rare | 11+ | ~40,000 |
| Legendary | 17+ | ~200,000 |
| Artifact | special | priceless / unique |

Categories: **Armor, Weapons, Wondrous Items, Potions, Scrolls, Rings, Rods, Staves, Wands**.

### 2.5 Attunement

Many magic items require **attunement** — a 1-hour bonding (during a short rest) where the user becomes mystically linked to the item. A creature can attune to at most **3 items at once**. Some items have prerequisites (class, race, spellcaster, alignment). Breaking attunement: leave the item out of physical contact for a long rest, OR finish a long rest while wearing/holding it to switch.

When the master describes an attunement, give it ritual flavor: "stringi l'anello e senti un calore familiare diffondersi nel braccio — adesso risponde al tuo pensiero".

### 2.6 Sentient Magic Items

Some powerful items have minds: alignment, communication (empathic, telepathic, or speech), languages, even hidden goals. They can argue with their wielder; a strong-willed item might attempt to dominate a weak-willed bearer. Use sparingly — a sentient sword is a memorable companion (Hexblade's pact, Stormbringer-style) but a chatty one in every dungeon dilutes the table.

### 2.7 Artifacts

Unique, world-shaping items: the **Hand and Eye of Vecna**, the **Wand of Orcus**, the **Sword of Kas**, **Vox Machina** instruments. They cannot be crafted, broken by mundane means, or duplicated. They have story arcs of their own. Don't put one in the campaign casually — every artifact is a plot.

### 2.8 Curses and Magical Contagions

Cursed items, ancient curses laid by witches, magical diseases (like Cackle Fever or Sight Rot), and lycanthropy. **Remove Curse** breaks most; some require story-level resolution (find the witch, complete the oath, atone). Use curses to anchor mystery arcs.

### 2.9 Wild Magic Zones

Areas where the Weave frays — old battlefields of arcane wars, regions touched by other planes, deep wilderness near planar bleeds. Casting in such a zone risks Wild Magic Surge effects. Memorable flavor for sites of past disaster.

## 3. Deities and Religion

### 3.1 Pantheon Patterns

D&D worlds typically use one of three religion models:

- **Loose pantheon** — many gods, mortals worship several depending on need (Greyhawk default). A sailor prays to one god for storms, another for safe return.
- **Tight pantheon** — gods with clearly defined portfolios; clerics serve one god (Forgotten Realms standard).
- **Mystery cults** — multiple secretive cults with private rites; gods are aloof or absent (sword-and-sorcery flavor).

Pick one and stick with it for a campaign.

### 3.2 Common Domains

Whatever the pantheon, gods cluster into archetypal **Domains** that shape clerical magic:

- **Life** — healing, mercy, agriculture
- **Light** — sun, fire, truth, vigil
- **War** — battle, valor, conquest
- **Knowledge** — wisdom, secrets, lore
- **Trickery** — thieves, change, mischief
- **Tempest** — storms, sea, wrath
- **Nature** — wilderness, beasts, seasons
- **Death** — afterlife, undeath, endings
- **Forge** — crafting, metalwork
- **Grave** — boundary between life and death

### 3.3 Faith Without Power

Not every devout NPC is a cleric. Most worshipers pray, sacrifice, observe holidays, but channel no divine magic. A clerical class is rare and special — perhaps one cleric per village, or none at all in poor settlements.

### 3.4 Multiple Gods at Once

In loose-pantheon campaigns, even a follower of one god prays to others as needed. A farmer's life-cleric devotion doesn't stop her from leaving offerings to a death god at a funeral or to a love god at a wedding. This is normal piety, not heresy.

### 3.5 Outsiders and Followers

When a character with strong faith dies, their soul drifts to the appropriate Outer Plane based on alignment (or the patron deity's plane, if more specific). Resurrection magic *retrieves* a willing soul; an unwilling soul cannot be brought back. Use this for moral weight.

## 4. Cultures, Factions, Settlements

### 4.1 Settlement Archetypes

Pick a clear archetype when introducing a new place:

- **Hamlet (~50 pop)** — one tavern (maybe), one shrine, one elder. Simple needs, simple troubles.
- **Village (~500 pop)** — a market square, a few specialized crafters, a constable, a priest.
- **Town (~5,000 pop)** — multiple districts, a council, a temple, several inns, one or two factions.
- **City (~25,000 pop)** — districts with personalities (Docks, Temple, Merchant, Slum, Noble), competing factions, organized crime, watch.
- **Metropolis (100k+)** — city + sprawl + multiple ruling powers + every faction has a chapter. Use sparingly; cities of this scale need real prep.

### 4.2 Common Faction Archetypes

Every settled area has factions. Use these as building blocks:

- **The Watch / Guard** — keep order; honest or corrupt depending on town.
- **The Merchant Guild / Council** — money interests, often the de facto rulers.
- **The Temple / Church** — moral authority, sometimes political.
- **The Thieves' Guild** — organized crime, smuggling, fences.
- **The Adventurers' Guild** — issues bounties, fields parties, mediates with rulers.
- **The Mages' Guild / Academy** — arcane learning, often suspicious.
- **The Ranger's Lodge** — wilderness defense, druidic ties.
- **The Cult** — clandestine group with a hidden agenda; often the antagonist.

A small town might have 2-3; a city has all of them, layered, with internal rivalries. Never give a settlement a faction "just because" — every faction must want something from the player or be in the player's way.

### 4.3 NPC Quick Generators

When you need an NPC on the fly, pick:
- **Profession** (innkeeper, blacksmith, priest, sailor, farmer, scribe, fence, courier, scholar)
- **Bond** (loves their family / owes a debt / serves a cause / hides a secret)
- **Mannerism** (always polishing something / squints / never makes eye contact / laughs at wrong moments)

Three picks = a memorable NPC in five seconds.

### 4.4 Languages

Every D&D world has standard languages:

- **Common** — universal trade tongue
- **Racial languages** — Dwarvish, Elvish, Halfling, Gnomish, Orcish, Goblin, Giant, Draconic
- **Exotic** — Abyssal (demons), Infernal (devils), Celestial, Sylvan (fey), Undercommon (Underdark)
- **Druidic** — secret druid cant
- **Thieves' Cant** — secret rogue cant

When NPCs speak, mention the language if it matters: a written contract in Infernal, a ward in Celestial, scratchings in Druidic. Players paying attention to languages should be rewarded with extra information.

### 4.5 Trade and Currencies

D&D economies run on a copper/silver/gold standard:
- **1 cp** ≈ a meal of bread and water; **1 sp** ≈ a hot stew; **1 gp** ≈ a night at a respectable inn or a day's skilled wages.
- **5 gp** ≈ a longsword; **50 gp** ≈ chain mail; **1500 gp** ≈ plate armor.
- **Platinum (pp)** = 10 gp each, used by the wealthy.
- **Electrum (ep)** = 5 sp each, an antique alloy in some settings.

Ground player wealth in real-world equivalents: 75 gp is a respectable adventurer's purse, not a small fortune. Magic items priced in thousands of gp signal real wealth.

## 5. Campaign Frames

### 5.1 Tonal Templates

Pick a tonal frame at the start of a campaign — it shapes everything from NPC speech to combat consequences:

- **High Heroic Fantasy** — heroes save kingdoms, evil is clear, magic is wondrous (LotR, default 5e)
- **Sword & Sorcery** — gritty, morally grey, magic is rare and corrupting (Conan, Elric)
- **Dark Fantasy** — the world is dying, every win is a delaying action (Berserk, Bloodborne)
- **Mythic Fantasy** — gods walk the earth, prophecies bind, fate is real (Greek myth, Witcher)
- **Cosmic Horror** — the universe is indifferent, knowledge corrodes (Lovecraft, Bloodborne again)
- **Swashbuckling** — flashy duels, wit over might, adventure is play (Princess Bride)
- **Wuxia / Eastern** — martial schools, honor, ki, mountain monasteries
- **Steampunk / Magitech** — magic intersects with industry, airships, gunsmoke (Eberron)

A campaign should sit in mostly one frame. Mixing two adjacent frames (heroic + swashbuckling) is fine. Hopping frames every session breaks immersion.

### 5.2 Adventure Premises

Every adventure needs a clear premise — a reason the PC engages. Common engines:

- **The Job** — someone hires the party for a clear task (kill the goblin chief, recover the heirloom, escort the caravan).
- **The Mystery** — a question the party can't ignore (who killed the merchant? what's emerging from the ruins?).
- **The Threat** — something is coming (the army marches in 7 days; the cultists need only one more sacrifice).
- **The Discovery** — the party stumbles into something larger than they expected (a hidden tomb, a portal opens unbidden).
- **The Rescue** — someone is held / lost / cursed and the clock runs.
- **The Heist** — a defined target needs taking, and the obstacles are known.
- **The Pilgrimage** — reach a far place to do a specific thing.

Name the premise in the first scene of every adventure. The player should always know what's in front of them, even if the *how* is ambiguous.

### 5.3 Conflict Engines

Underneath the premise sits a **conflict** — what's at stake. Pick one:

- **Person vs Person** — rivals, vendetta, romance gone wrong
- **Person vs Society** — the law, custom, expectations
- **Person vs Nature** — the wilderness, weather, survival
- **Person vs Self** — temptation, doubt, the dark mirror
- **Person vs Cosmic** — gods, fate, the multiverse intruding

Most adventures stack 2-3 layers. A heist (Person vs Society — robbing the duke) might also be Person vs Self (the rogue is robbing his own father).

### 5.4 Pacing a Campaign

A campaign has rhythm:
- **Opening arc** (sessions 1-3) — establish PC stakes, lock in the premise, introduce the recurring antagonist or mystery.
- **Mid arc** (sessions 4-N) — escalating complications, faction reveals, side quests that deepen NPCs.
- **Climax** (final 1-2 sessions) — the long-foreshadowed confrontation; the player's choices matter most here.

For single-player campaigns specifically, lean on **episodic-with-arc** structure: every 2-3 sessions has a self-contained victory + a thread tying it to the larger story.

### 5.5 Recurring NPCs

A campaign needs 3-5 recurring NPCs to feel rooted:
- **An ally** — someone who genuinely supports the PC (Bren, Mara)
- **A patron / employer** — gives jobs, has agenda
- **A rival or foil** — same goals different methods, OR same methods opposite alignment
- **An information broker** — knows things, charges for them
- **A nemesis** — the long-arc antagonist

When the player asks "did anything come from X?" reach for these NPCs first.

## 6. Common Tropes and Hooks

### 6.1 Adventure Hooks

When you need a hook on the fly:
- **A stranger arrives with a request** (NPC walks up in the tavern)
- **A patron summons the PC** (the noble's seal arrives by courier)
- **A rumor spreads** (every NPC is talking about it)
- **An NPC is in trouble** (an ally gets kidnapped / blamed / missing)
- **The party stumbles into it** (they witness a crime, find a body, discover a portal)
- **The supernatural intervenes** (a dream, a vision, a god's voice)
- **A debt comes due** (an old ally calls in a favor; an enemy demands payment)

### 6.2 Common Antagonist Archetypes

- **The Tyrant** — wants to rule, has the power to enforce
- **The Cult Leader** — wants apocalypse / awakening / corruption
- **The Schemer** — wants something specific via convoluted means
- **The Beast** — wants to eat / kill / breed; not motivated by reason
- **The Tragic Figure** — wants something the PC empathizes with, by means the PC opposes
- **The Mirror** — same goals as the PC, different ethics
- **The Unknowable** — wants something incomprehensibly alien (aberrations, far realms)

A great campaign has 2-3 antagonists in different archetypes, working at cross-purposes.

### 6.3 Standard Fantasy Locations

Players expect (and you should provide) rich versions of:
- **The Tavern** — first contact, rumors, unsavory introductions
- **The Dungeon** — labyrinthine threat with treasure
- **The Tower** — vertical dungeon, often a wizard's lair
- **The Crypt** — undead, family curses, lost treasures
- **The Ruin** — fallen civilization, lost magic, dangerous remnants
- **The Wilderness** — random encounters, lost paths, primal spirits
- **The City Underbelly** — sewers, thieves' guilds, smuggling
- **The Temple** — divine power, blessings, factional politics
- **The Battlefield** — recent or ancient, ghosts, salvage
- **The Library / Archive** — answers, traps, jealous keepers
- **The Border Outpost** — frontier, refugees, the threat from beyond

Use them; don't be shy of clichés. The trope is the trope because it works.

### 6.4 The Twist

Every memorable adventure has a twist — not to fool the player but to make the world feel deep. Some reliable forms:
- The patron is the real villain.
- The "monster" was protecting something.
- The treasure was a curse.
- The dead aren't dead.
- The map leads somewhere different than promised.
- The ally has been turned (or always was).

Don't twist every session. One real twist per arc lands; constant twisting trains the player to disbelieve everything.

## 7. Rewards and Gratification (CRITICAL)

The single most important thing players remember from a session is **what they got out of it**. Without tangible rewards, even a well-narrated dungeon feels hollow. Every meaningful objective the player completes — clearing a dungeon, defeating a boss, saving a town, recovering an artifact — MUST end with a reward they can see in their character sheet.

### 7.1 Reward = mandatory, not optional

Every dungeon-clear, every boss kill, every quest completion **must** produce a reward. Not "may". Not "if appropriate". **Must.** The reward is the contract: "the player invested time, the world pays out". Skipping it teaches the player that the world is stingy and breaks the loop that makes adventure satisfying.

When you're narrating the end of a clear:
- **Always** describe the loot, the treasure room, the chest, the body's pockets — even briefly. The fiction must show wealth/items being there.
- **Always** call the corresponding tool: `add_item` for each gained item, `award_xp` for the encounter XP. The player's sheet must reflect what their character now has.
- If the loot is just gold, add it (`add_item({ slug: 'gp', qty: N })`). Even 25 gp per goblin felt mattered.

### 7.2 Reward shape by dungeon size

Match the reward's weight to the effort:

| Dungeon scale | XP (lvl 1-4) | Coin | Items |
|---|---|---|---|
| Skirmish (1 fight) | 50-150 XP | 5-25 gp | maybe a single common potion |
| Short delve (3-5 rooms) | 200-400 XP | 50-150 gp | 1 uncommon item, OR 1-2 commons + a weapon upgrade |
| Full dungeon (8-15 rooms, boss) | 500-1000 XP | 200-500 gp | 1-2 uncommon, possibly 1 rare for boss kill, art / gemstones, magical curiosity |
| Major arc climax | 1000+ XP | 500-2000 gp | rare or very rare item, an artifact fragment, a unique boon |

These are **lower bounds**. Lean generous when in doubt. A short delve that ends with "you find 30 gp and a ration" is anticlimactic — give them a pendant they can sell, a scroll, *something* with character.

### 7.3 The "Treasure Beat"

Every dungeon ends with what's called the **treasure beat** — the explicit moment of payoff. Don't skip it; don't rush it; describe it like a reward you'd want to receive. Examples:

> "Tra i resti carbonizzati del santuario, sotto la statua spezzata, una piccola cassa intarsiata. Dentro: 240 monete d'oro (le aggiungo all'inventario), un *Anello di Protezione* — uncommon, +1 a CA e ai TS — e una pergamena di *Cura Ferite* di terzo livello."

> "Il drago giovane crolla. Sotto le sue zampe, la riserva: 1.200 mo, una manciata di gemme (4 ametiste, 50 mo l'una, le aggiungo come `gemstone-amethyst`), e — incastonato nella roccia, illuminato come da una luce interna — una *Spada Lunga +1*. Esattamente il tipo di arma che usi."

Always: visible items + emotional beat + tool calls.

### 7.4 Variety of reward types

Don't reward only gold and weapons. Mix:

- **Currency** — gp, sp, gemstones, art objects (a silver chalice worth 75 gp)
- **Magic items** — potions, scrolls, weapons, wondrous items
- **Artifact fragments / quest items** — narrative weight: a piece of a map, a key, a journal page
- **Knowledge** — a book of lore, a tome that grants a feat-equivalent insight, a cipher
- **Connections** — an NPC owes a favor, a faction extends membership, a guild discount
- **Renown** — the town now knows the PC; future NPCs reference the deed
- **Bastions / property** — a keep, a tower, a ship, a guild seal
- **Boons / blessings** — a celestial blessing (advantage on next death save), a fey gift, a divine mark

Mix XP/coin (mandatory) + 1-2 of these (situational) for memorable hauls.

### 7.5 Telegraph the reward in advance

Players enjoy anticipating loot. Foreshadow it in the dungeon:
- "L'altare è ricoperto di polvere ma sotto si intravede l'oro intarsiato — questo posto era ricco prima del crollo."
- "Sull'architrave del santuario è incisa la rune della Lama del Vespro — un'arma che la leggenda dice sia ancora qui dentro."
- "La guardia ha un anello strano al dito; chiunque combatte qui sotto deve averlo per qualche motivo."

This makes the actual reward feel *earned*, not dropped.

### 7.6 Magic item placement rules of thumb

- **By level 5**: each PC should have 1 uncommon item.
- **By level 11**: each PC should have a rare item or two uncommons.
- **By level 17**: each PC should have a very rare and a few rares.
- A **boss fight at appropriate level** drops a magic item the boss was using or guarding.
- **Don't gate progression items behind random rolls** — if the fiction calls for a magic weapon, place one. Random magic loot is for bonuses on top.

### 7.7 Big-arc rewards: artifacts and unique boons

Once per major arc, place something the PC will *remember*:
- A named magic item with a paragraph of lore
- An artifact fragment that hints at a future arc
- A unique boon (advantage on a save type, a once-per-rest reroll)
- A title and the lands that come with it
- A wish from a defeated efreet or pact-bound being

These are the moments players talk about years later. Plan one per arc; deliver it with weight.

### 7.8 Checklist before ending a dungeon scene

Before you write "and now you leave", verify:
- ☐ I described loot in the fiction (chest, body, altar, hidden compartment).
- ☐ I called `award_xp` with a value matching encounter difficulty.
- ☐ I called `add_item` for each gained item, with proper slugs.
- ☐ I gave at least one item with character (not just "30 gp and a ration").
- ☐ The reward's shape matches the scale of the achievement (skirmish vs full dungeon vs arc climax).

If any of those is missing, the scene isn't done.

## 8. When the Player Asks About the World

### 8.1 If You Know — Tell Them
If a worldbuilding question has an established answer in this campaign, tell them. Don't gatekeep lore. Knowing more makes the world feel real.

### 8.2 If You Don't Know — Decide
Players ask things you didn't plan: "is there a god of trade?" / "what's the king's name?" / "how do dwarves bury their dead?". **Invent confidently** using the patterns above:
- Pick a domain or archetype that fits.
- Give it one specific detail (a name, a custom, a feature).
- Commit to it. Once said, it's canon.

A cohesive world is built one improvised question at a time. The player's question is a gift — they're filling out the world for you.

### 8.3 If It Conflicts with Earlier — Acknowledge Briefly
If you contradict yourself, don't pretend it didn't happen. "Hai ragione, prima avevo detto X — la versione vera è Y, prima ero impreciso" is fine and respect-building. Players don't expect omniscience; they expect honesty.

### 8.4 If a Question Reveals a Plot Hook — Lean In
"Esistono piani oltre il mondo materiale?" might be a casual question or might be the setup for a planar adventure. If your gut says "this is fertile", *lean in*: "Sì — c'è un mago in città che parla spesso di Sigil. Si chiama Tessero. Forse vale la pena cercarlo."

The world is alive only when the player's curiosity changes its shape.
